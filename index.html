Required Step

How to Create a Gmail App Password

Our tool uses your own Gmail to send emails — securely and without third-party servers.
Google no longer allows regular passwords for apps like this, so you need a special 16-digit App Password.
Important: You must enable 2-Step Verification first.

Step 1 Enable 2-Step Verification (if not already on)

  1. Go to your Google Account
  2. Click Security in the left menu
  3. Under "How you sign in to Google", click 2-Step Verification
  4. Follow the prompts to turn it on (use your phone number or authenticator app)
  5. Complete verification — you'll get a code on your phone

Step 2 Generate your App Password

  1. Go directly to myaccount.google.com/apppasswords
  2. Sign in if asked
  3. In "Select app", choose Other (Custom name)
  4. Type something like LinkMail Bulk Mail
  5. Click Generate
  6. Copy the 16-digit code that appears (example: abcd efgh ijkl mnop)

⚠️ This code shows only once. Paste it into LinkMail when asked — never share it.

Quick Tips

  • Use this App Password only in LinkMail
  • Your real Gmail password stays safe
  • If you lose the code → just generate a new one (old one stops working)
  • Revoke anytime from the same page if needed

Why we need this

Google blocked "less secure apps" years ago. App Passwords let tools like ours connect safely without ever seeing your real password — and without using paid SMTP services.

Pro Tip

Find the right email convention first

Different companies use different formats. Guessing wrong means every email bounces. Here's how to find it in 2 minutes — for free.

Step-by-step: Find email from the company website

1

Go to the company's official website and find their "Contact Us" or "About" page. Many companies list a general HR or tech email here.

2

Search Google for: "@company.com" HR recruiter email site:linkedin.com — LinkedIn profiles sometimes reveal public work emails.

3

Check the company's press / newsroom page. Press contact emails often follow the same company-wide convention.

4

Look at their GitHub profile, blog posts, or open-source contributions — devs often commit with their work email visible.

5

Once you find even one email like john.doe@company.com, you've identified the pattern. Apply it to all names.

The 6 most common corporate email formats

firstname.lastname

e.g. john.doe@company.com — Most common

firstnamelastname

e.g. johndoe@company.com

firstname_lastname

e.g. john_doe@company.com

f.lastname

e.g. j.doe@company.com — Startups often use this

firstname

e.g. john@company.com — Small teams

flastname

e.g. jdoe@company.com

💡 Always confirm with a real email from the company before sending bulk mail. Guessing without verification leads to bounces.

Verify before you send — free methods

Use Hunter.io (free plan)

Enter the company domain at hunter.io — it shows you the email pattern used by that company and up to 25 emails for free.

Google search: @company.com filetype:pdf

PDFs like annual reports and whitepapers often contain author emails with the standard format.

Check LinkedIn "Contact info"

Some LinkedIn profiles have public contact emails visible in the "Contact info" section. This is a direct source of truth.

⚠️ Why this matters: Sending to wrong formats causes hard bounces which can damage your Gmail sender reputation. Always verify the pattern on at least one real email before going bulk.
